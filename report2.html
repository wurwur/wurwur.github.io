Introduction

This report presents my mathematical and algorithmic approach to the traffic junction simulation model engine. All the code is implemented in Python.

from problem import solution

I have used fundamental traffic flow theories following the Lighthill-Whitham-Richards (LWR) model. The green light allocation is based on Greenshields’ fundamental diagram, the queue updates make use of a modified Webster’s signal timing formula whereas the control delay is approximated by Delay Model Functions (HCM 2000). The combination of which provides an accurate (enough) simulation with very limited time complexity TrafficSimulator∈Θ(3600×4)TrafficSimulator \in \Theta(3600 \times 4)TrafficSimulator∈Θ(3600×4) The constant complexity provided by the mathematical framework allows the backend to run extremely quickly, and also be consistant across runs (same input ↔ same output, unless the mario n64 speedrun accident was to happen again).

The TrafficSimulatortm algorithm (or object depending on who you ask) computes vehicule delays and queue lengths, important metrics and runs the mathematical simulation under multiple assumptions (stated clearly later) based on user’s input.

Traffic Flow Modeling (its the fun stuff)

The Lighthill-Whitham-Richards (LWR) Model

The LWR (named after some mathematicians who do stuff way beyond my comprehension) model is a first-order macroscopic traffic flow model given by:

∂k∂t+∂q∂x=0, \begin{equation} \frac{\partial k}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial q}{\partial x} = 0, \end{equation} ∂t∂k​+∂x∂q​=0,​​

where:

  • kkk is the traffic density (veh/km),
  • qqq is the traffic flow (veh/h),
  • xxx is the spatial coordinate,
  • ttt is time.

The fundamental relation is: q=kv,\begin{equation} q = k v, \end{equation}q=kv,​​ where vvv is the velocity of vehicles.

The LWR model (111) is borrowed from fluid mechanics (flashback of my explanation in the first meeting).

Intuition

Note: During the first meeting, I explained that my initial intuition was to model the junction using fluid mechanics. As the flow, speed, vehicule saturation and general traffic density of each direction are consistent; we can imagine a model as follows:

Take 4 independant jugs (N,E,S,W)(N, E, S, W)(N,E,S,W). Each one has a total capacity SSS, a faucet on top with a flow fff, and a hole at the bottom of size hhh. In my general intuition, I imagined:
fff to be a function of the traffic flow and the priority (and anything that relates to the rate at which vehicules come in).
hhh to be a function of the number of lanes and speed of vehicules (what relates to vehicules leaving).
SSS to be a limit based on fff (the input flow) and hhh (the out-going flow)

At any moment of time ttt, one (and only one) of the holes must be open (following hhh), liquid thus exiting the jug. At every moment ttt, the water in the jugs gets fuller from the faucet following fff. The problem is now pretty simple: across a fixed cycle of time c>0c>0c>0, find the optimal sequence of faucets (hN,hE,hS,hW)(h_N, h_E, h_S, h_W)(hN​,hE​,hS​,hW​) such that the jugs are as empty as possible across the full cycle. By intuition still, I imagined a system of four 2nd degree different equations that would need to be minimized, which is hard but do-able. I imagined that by hard-coding the equations and a solver, we could not only have a working solution, but also have a pretty easily expandible and parametrized (not sure this word exists) solution. From that cycle of green/red lights, all metrics could be pretty easily computed, and from the metrics a final score could be computed.

If at first (before doing anything), the limit SSS grows to infinity, it implies that the traffic incoming is greater that what can be out-going even by allocating all the cycle time to that unique jug: the maximum saturation of the direction is exceeded by the incoming traffic, and the jugs overflow. And thus, all the metrics (avg wait time, max wait time and max queue length) would grow to infinity. Infinity and differential equations are not good friends, so we disregard the set of inputs and mark it as impossible.

This initial intuition was found a bit too hard to implement on its own without making use of any pre-existing research, and also hard to prove that the solution is accurate for the problem we have (as much as I love engineering, relating cars to water is not obvious), but the idea remains more or less the same across the models used below. LWR applied to the junction

Properly speaking, it is a dynamic traffic flow model which relates the evolution of speed, density and flow over time based on conservation laws. However, we are assuming steady-state equilibrium (v=v(k) ∀k∈R+v = v(k) \space\forall k \in \R ^+v=v(k) ∀k∈R+, aka speed is constant for all densities).

The junction (or rather, each direction of the junction) is modelled as a fluid going on a pre-defined path. The fluid incoming is of constant (given) rate qqq. The fluid out-going is following a fixed path of length xxx. In our model, we can assume that the distance between every pair of entry/exit of the junction are equal. Therefore, for a linear progression from x0x_0x0​ to xmaxx_{max}xmax​, we can:

  • Compute the density kkk of the traffic at a moment ttt
  • Relate the evolution of density through a period Δt\Delta tΔt
  • Relate traffic flow to speed, traffic density and speed

You could probably (rightfully) tell me that solving a system of three unknown variables isn’t really do-able. But remember! We assume steady-state equilibrium: speed is a constant vvv, and qqq a parameter given by the user.
This information implies that (v,k)(v,k)(v,k) ALWAYS travels together alongside a space xxx. Which brings us over to equation (222), which gives us (still from the LWR model) a linear relationship between density, speed and flow :D

But we would work in a system where traffic isn’t constant. Red/Green light cycle implies that traffic doesn’t run solely based on time’s linearity. We need to put our relation (222) on steroids.

Application of the model

From the saturation flow and traffic flow, we can compute the weight of each direction in the system (the bigger qS\frac{q}{S}Sq​, the more green time), from the weight we can compute it’s green light cycle, from the green light cycle we can compute our metrics. Yipee :)

Traffic Signal Optimization (literally what I just talked about)

Webster’s Green Time Allocation

Webster’s formula estimates optimal cycle splits for each phase, considering flow (qqq) and saturation flow (SSS) (which we have expressed in equation (555)) as:

gi=wi∑wC\begin{equation} g_i = \frac{w_i}{\sum w} C \end{equation}gi​=∑wwi​​C​​ where:

  • CCC is the total cycle time,
  • wiw_iwi​ is the weight of each phase, computed as: wi=Pi×qiSi\begin{equation} w_i = \frac{P_i \times q_i}{S_i} \end{equation}wi​=Si​Pi​×qi​​​​

Thus, phases with higher priority or lower saturation get more green time after normalization of weigths.

What does that mean? It means we can set a cycle of green lights (in my code, I decided that all lanes should get a green light across a 90s90s90s cycle, value can be freely changed as long as it is non-null positive), use weights computed based on all our magic maths and yipee, we can get optimal green times.

The queue length is updated as: Qt+1=max⁡(Qt−Dt,0)+At\begin{equation} Q_{t+1} = \max(Q_t - D_t, 0) + A_t \end{equation}Qt+1​=max(Qt​−Dt​,0)+At​​​ where AtA_tAt​ is arrivals and DtD_tDt​ is departures per unit time.

Those two models will allow us, across a defined cycle (of 1 hour, chosen to fit the given flow rates), to visualize the evolution of traffic based on our green light cycle model (666)).
The random and overflow models apply to interrupted traffic. But we want one with a cycle. So we code a simulation such that:

  • If the light is green, traffic is dispatched based of those two models
  • If the light is red, traffic accumulates based on LWR’s model and Webster’s weighted green-light allocation.

Why use a delay approximation?

Why HCM2000 when I could just remove from existence a car based on a fixed timer? Because cars aren’t magic. We can make assumptions on our simulation engine, and have already made plenty, so why cut corners? By associating the uniform delay model (which assumes stable flow), the random delay (which implements randomized parameters such as vehicle size, still assuming perfect drivers) and the overflow parameter (which remembers residual queues from previous red/green cycle); we get a final simulation core that takes into account most of what we need while making minimal unrealistic assumptions.

Normal Distribution of Pedestrians

This one’s a wild ride, hold onto your calculators.

We want to find the average time WWW at which each pedestrian crosses the road, including waking time and waiting for the good cycle.
To do so, we imagine a model in which pedestrians arrive (uniformly), and either “catch” a crossing time that allows them to cross, or wait until the next one. We could just add yet another check on the simulation core, but that would mean adding way more steps to the computation than i’d like. So we use maths and make it a normal distribution.

“ooooh but harm why not use a uniform distribution??” would say someone who’s BORING. Technically speaking, since their arrival is uniform, we could. And I am using that fact for some simplifications. But a normal distribution allows the implementation of stochastic factors (reaction time, variable walking speed etc etc) which could be useful in the future.
One could also argue that the total crossing time being jittered by the arrival makes the core approximately Gaussian. However, it’s fun to do maths. Also I think it is less computationally intensive and more precise than a uniform distribution but don’t quote me on that one.

Algorithmic Aspects of TrafficSimulator

The TrafficSimulator class/object manages the simulation by iteratively updating the queue state and the computing aspect of delay metrics. The main components are basically:

Queue Evolution

For each phase, the queue length is updated every second (remember, we work across a 1h cycle, or 3600s):

  • If the phase is green, vehicles discharge at a rate: D=Sg3600\begin{equation} D = \frac{S g}{3600} \end{equation}D=3600Sg​​​
  • If the phase is red, vehicles arrive at a rate: A=v3600\begin{equation} A = \frac{v}{3600} \end{equation}A=3600v​​​
  • The updated queue is: Qt+1=max⁡(Qt−D,0)+A\begin{equation} Q_{t+1} = \max(Q_t - D, 0) + A \end{equation}Qt+1​=max(Qt​−D,0)+A​​

Simulation Loop

For each time step ttt (1s increments over 3600s):

  1. Check if the phase is green or red.
  2. Update queue lengths based on the current cycle (either discharge or arrival).
  3. Store delay dtd_tdt​ and queue length QtQ_tQt​ in history.

Metric Computation

After simulation, compute:

  • Average wait time: ddd
  • Max wait time: max⁡(dt)\max(d_t)max(dt​)
  • Max queue length: max⁡(Qt)\max(Q_t)max(Qt​)

class TrafficConfig:
    def __init__(self, cycle_time=90, v_max=40, road_length=0.00365, lanes=None, priorities=None, pedestrian_demand=None, phases=["N", "S", "E", "W"], traffic_demand=None, pedestrian_speed=5):
        self.cycle_time = cycle_time
        self.v_max = v_max  # km/h
        self.road_length = road_length  # km
        self.lanes = lanes or {phase: 1 for phase in phases}
        self.priorities = priorities or {phase: 1 for phase in phases}
        self.pedestrian_demand = pedestrian_demand or {phase: 0 for phase in phases}
        self.phases = phases
        self.traffic_demand = traffic_demand or {phase: 1000 for phase in self.phases}
        self.saturation_flows = self.calculate_saturation_flows()
        self.green_times = self.calculate_green_times()
        self.phase_road_length = {phase: lanes[phase] * road_length for phase in phases} # still in km
        self.pedestrian_speed = pedestrian_speed # km/h
        
    def calculate_saturation_flows(self):
        """Compute saturation flow using LWR model and Greenshields’ fundamental diagram"""
        k_j = 150  # Jam density (veh/km)
        saturation_flows = {}
        for phase in self.phases:
            L = self.lanes[phase]  # Number of lanes
            P = self.priorities[phase]  # Priority factor
            S = self.v_max * (k_j / 2) * L # LWR + Greenshields formula
            saturation_flows[phase] = S
        return saturation_flows

    def calculate_green_times(self):
        """Calculate weighted green times with priorities"""
        priority_levels = {1: "None", 2: "Low", 3: "Medium", 4: "High"}

        critical_ratio = sum(self.traffic_demand[phase] / self.saturation_flows[phase] for phase in self.phases)
       
        if critical_ratio > 1:
            raise ValueError("Critical flow ratio exceeds 1. Traffic demand is too high for the given saturation flow.")

        green_times = {}
        total_weight = sum((self.traffic_demand[phase] * self.priorities[phase]) / self.saturation_flows[phase] for phase in self.phases)

        print("\nConfiguration Parameters:")
        for phase in self.phases:
            weight = (self.traffic_demand[phase] * self.priorities[phase]) / self.saturation_flows[phase]
            normalized_weight = weight / total_weight  # Normalize

            print(f'Phase: {phase}\tWeight: {normalized_weight:.3f}\tPriority: {self.priorities[phase]} ({priority_levels[self.priorities[phase]]})\tLanes: {self.lanes[phase]}\tSaturation: {self.saturation_flows[phase]}\tDemand: {self.traffic_demand[phase]}')

            green_times[phase] = normalized_weight * self.cycle_time

        return green_times

# Delay Model Functions (HCM 2000)

def uniform_delay(C, gC, X):
    """Webster's uniform delay component"""
    return 0.5 * C * (1 - gC)**2 / (1 - min(1, X) * gC)

def random_delay(X, c, T=1):
    """Akcelik's random+overflow delay component"""
    if X <= 0.67 + (c * T) / 600:
        return 0
    return 900 * T * ((X - 1) + np.sqrt((X - 1)**2 + (8 * 0.5 * X) / (c * T)))

class TrafficSimulator:
    def __init__(self, config, traffic_demand):
        self.config = config
        self.traffic_demand = traffic_demand
        self.queues = {phase: 0.0 for phase in self.config.phases}
        self.delay_history = {phase: [] for phase in self.config.phases}
        self.queue_history = {phase: [] for phase in self.config.phases}

    def calculate_metrics(self, phase):
        """Calculate delay components using HCM2000 model"""
        v = self.traffic_demand[phase]
        c = self.config.saturation_flows[phase] * (self.config.green_times[phase] / self.config.cycle_time)
        X = v / c if c > 0 else 0
        
        d1 = uniform_delay(self.config.cycle_time, 
                          self.config.green_times[phase] / self.config.cycle_time, 
                          X)
        d2 = random_delay(X, c)
        return 0.9 * (d1 + d2)  # Webster's adjustment factor (constant i stole from studies by a much smarter guy)

    def update_queues(self, phase, elapsed_time):
        """Update queue state"""
        arrival_rate = self.traffic_demand[phase] / 3600
        discharge_rate = self.config.saturation_flows[phase] / 3600
        # pedestrian_arrival = self.config.pedestrian_demand[phase] / 3600
        # pedestrian_discharge = self.config.

        green_time = self.config.green_times[phase]
        cycle_portion = elapsed_time % self.config.cycle_time
        
        if cycle_portion < green_time:
            self.queues[phase] = max(self.queues[phase] - discharge_rate + arrival_rate, 0)
        else:
            self.queues[phase] += arrival_rate
            
        self.queue_history[phase].append(self.queues[phase])
        self.delay_history[phase].append(self.queues[phase] / arrival_rate if arrival_rate > 0 else 0)

    def run_simulation(self, duration=3600):
        """Run simulation for a specified cycle"""
        print("\nDelay times (seconds/veh):")
        for phase in self.config.phases:
            print(f"{phase}: {self.calculate_metrics(phase):.2f} seconds")

        for t in range(duration):
            for phase in self.config.phases:
                self.update_queues(phase, t)

    def expected_wait_time(self, pedestrian_demand, phase_road_length, pedestrian_speed, green_times, phase):
        """Calculate pedestrian average wait time"""
        if not pedestrian_demand:
            return 0
        
        pedestrian_speed /= 3600 # km/s
        
        t_cross = phase_road_length / pedestrian_speed # distance to cross (km)
        
        # for t_g:

        phase_green = green_times[phase]
        mu = phase_green / 2 # mean
        sigma = math.sqrt(phase_green ** 2 / 12) # variance

        alpha = phase_green - t_cross
        if alpha < 0:
            print("Error: pedestrians dont have time to cross the full road s_1")
            return 0

        Z = (alpha - mu) / sigma
        
        phi_alpha = (1 / math.sqrt(2 * math.pi)) * math.exp(-0.5 * Z ** 2)
        Phi_alpha = 0.5 * (1 + math.erf(Z / math.sqrt(2))) 
        
        E_W_green = (alpha - mu) * Phi_alpha + sigma * phi_alpha

        # for \bar{t_g}:

        phase_red = sum([p for t,p in green_times.items() if not t==phase]) # update with left turn lanes to remove the counter-clockwise phase too if it has a left turn lane

        mu = phase_red / 2 # mean
        sigma = math.sqrt(phase_red ** 2 / 12) # variance
        alpha = phase_red - t_cross
        if alpha < 0:
            print("Error: pedestrians dont have time to cross the full road s_2")
            return 0

        Z = (alpha - mu) / sigma
        
        phi_alpha = (1 / math.sqrt(2 * math.pi)) * math.exp(-0.5 * Z ** 2)
        Phi_alpha = 0.5 * (1 + math.erf(Z / math.sqrt(2))) 

        E_W_red = (alpha - mu) * Phi_alpha + sigma * phi_alpha

        W_total = max(E_W_green + E_W_red, 0) + t_cross
        
        # print(f'green: {E_W_green}, red: {E_W_red}')
        return W_total


    def pedestrian_metrics(self, phase):
        """Compute and format pedestrian metrics"""
        p_avg_wait = self.expected_wait_time(self.config.pedestrian_demand[phase], self.config.phase_road_length[phase], self.config.pedestrian_speed, self.config.green_times, phase)
        p_max_wait = self.config.cycle_time + (3600 * 2 * self.config.phase_road_length[phase] / self.config.pedestrian_speed) if p_avg_wait else 0
        p_max_crowd = math.ceil(self.config.pedestrian_demand[phase] * p_max_wait / 3600) if p_avg_wait else 0

        return {"p_avg_wait": p_avg_wait,
                "p_max_wait": p_max_wait,
                "p_max_crowd": p_max_crowd}

    def get_metrics(self):
        """Calculate performance metrics"""
        metrics = {}
        for phase in self.config.phases:

            p = self.pedestrian_metrics(phase)
            q = np.array(self.queue_history[phase])
            d = np.array(self.delay_history[phase])
            # print(self.queue_history[phase])

            metrics[phase] = {
                'avg_wait': self.calculate_metrics(phase),
                'max_wait': np.max(d),
                'max_queue': np.max(q),
                'pedestrian': p
            }

        return metrics

        score[phase] = 4 + 100/(100+m['avg_wait']+m['max_wait']+m['max_queue'])
    return score

Future steps

  • Implement left-turn lanes, which allows two opposite directions to be on at the same time while ensuring proper traffic regulation.

    • Implement user input for the different directed flows.
    • In the simulation core, separate the discharge rate such that only non-left turn lanes are considered
    • If opposite direction both have left turn lanes, allow their green light cycles to overlap (considering the optimisation factor of the mathematical framework, they would logically have a total overlap equal to the greatest individual cycle lengths of both).
    • If only direction (dld_ldl​) has a left turn lane, but the opposite direction (drd_rdr​) does not, allow flow on dtd_tdt​ when drd_rdr​ is on a green cycle

- Pedestrian crossing with specific metrics computations
- Allow user to input pedestrian flow for each direction in which pedestrian crossing is to be allowed
- Compute the inverse of the simulation (pedestrian discharge rate is on red lights)
- Run the same metrics again (average wait time, maximum wait time, max “crowd” size)

  • Bus/Cycle lanes with independant lane
    • Simply add a lane to the simulation, but make it exclusive to bus/cycle. The exclusivity of the lane would follow the same logic as a left turn (where only a specific subset of the total flow of the phase is allowed on that lane).
    • Either include bus lanes results into the total metrics, or impelement a separate output metric following the same template
  • If given time, train a neural network based on real-world data to make use of long short-term memory for way more accurate results (notably for a much more accurate distribution of green-time)
    • This point is only (and only) if enough time is available.
    • Gather real world data from online sources
    • Train a neural network agaisnt a pure discrete-time implementation and our mathematical framework
    • The output of the neural network would be the green-light cycle. No other parts of the simulation will be changed
    • A neural network could be used for a “real world” mode that makes absolute minimal assuptions of ideal conditions. The use of long short-term memory could help notice patterns (eg: change of density, reaction time of drivers being slower the longer the queue, accidents slowing doing the entire traffic etc) and make a (if succesful) even more accurate version of the green-time allocation (which is, thus far, the part of the framework that is the least accurate)

Assumptions made

  • Traffic flow is consistent and uniform
  • Speed is constant
  • Infinite Acceleration and Deceleration
  • All drivers are perfect (always in the right lane, perfect reaction time, no accidents)
  • All traffic lights switch instantly from green to red
  • All pairs of entry and exits have the same distance
