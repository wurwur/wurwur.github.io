A modern approach to nuclear waste burial site safety assessment through hydro-geomechanical and radiological modelling

Abstract

Assessing the long-term safety of nuclear waste disposal in regard to its biosphere impact remains one of the most critical challenges in the continued use of nuclear fission energy production.

While deep geological burial has been proven effective and safe under a set of clearly defined guidelines; however, current site assessment methods are often hindered by the usage of fragmented tools, heuristic approximations and heavy reliance on manual processing and intuition, thus introducing plausible human error failure points and oversights, heavily impeding of assessment timelines.

This project proposes a modern, deterministic approach to a simulation framework for assessing the safety of nuclear waste burial sites through the combined use of hydro-geo mechanical and quantum radiological modelling within a 3D geological context.

The deterministic and physically grounded simulation approach emphasizes a modular design, capable of accounting for key processes such as groundwater dynamics, heterogeneity of geological media, thermal transfers and radionuclides dispersion. The framework aims to align with current safety standards and produce a reliable, efficient, and extensible simulation engine that aids nuclear safety engineers in making rigorous evidence-based decisions, improving the accuracy, confidence and time-requirements necessary in the assessment of potential burial sites.

Testing, validation, rigorousness and viability assessment

Pick a strict incremental approach (just passed the CS261 exam, as you can tell). Start by stating each component that could be in the final engine, and their importance:

  • Critical, which is necessary and without which the engine is pointless
  • Essential, without which the engine is too bare
  • Possible, which adds coveted but not essential aspects to the engine
  • Superfluous, which adds elements of minimal importance

From this list of components, build a time-line requirements based on dependencies (ex: hydrological module requires the 3d data to be readable and parsed) & importance (ex: a live 3d view of the data does not require any dependencies, but is extremely superfluous, so it would be at the very end of the time-line).

For each step of production (assuming a list of modules):

  1. Start with the theory (algorithmic, geo-mechanical…),
  2. From which a SINGLE clearly defined and strategized development requirement will stem
  3. Associated to the requirement, build a testing-suite (mathematical equations of known values, real world data on which testing has already been completed…). This testing-suite will be appended to the list of all previous testing-suite up until that point (ensuring that no new module affects a pre-defined & tested module)
  4. Proceed with the implementation while taking notes of every step (even if disregarded),
  5. Apply the full testing-suite to the new implementation
  6. If successful, proceed to the next module; if not, go back to step 1.

Risk mitigation, time management and failure strategy

The incremental approach ensures that even with the worst time management possible, a deliverable with its associated up-to-date report will be available (although the report will obviously require supervision and refinement).
