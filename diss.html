Ray-Tracing Gamma Transport Solver for the Assessment of Nuclear Waste Burial Safety

CS310 – Project Specification Report

Abstract

The safety assessment of nuclear waste burial remains one of the most critical aspects in the continued usage of fission energy. Gamma radiation undergoes many physical phenomena when interacting with matter such as absorption and scattering, all depending on many characteristics of the radiation and the matter itself. These interactions are modeled by the Linear Boltzman Transport Equations (LBTE). Current approaches to solving the LBTE are either computationally prohibitive or too innacurate. The aim of this project is to provide a new hybrid approach to approaching a specific set of instances of LBTEs with satisfiable and demonstrable accuracy within a reasonable run-time. Such an approach would exploit and apply ray-tracing specific mechanics and data structures to beams of photons. In addition, to give a structure on which the simulation engine can be applied to, a data-driven geological interpolation model based on BGS data and statistical interpretation modules will be implemented, allowing for a less abstract way of visualizing the simulation’s output in a physical sense.

1. Problem Context and Motivation

1.1 Background

Assessing the long-term safety of nuclear waste disposal in regard to its biosphere impact remains one of the most critical challenges in the continued use of nuclear fission energy production.

While deep geological burial has been proven effective and safe under a set of clearly defined guidelines, current site assessment methods are often hindered by the usage of fragmented tools, heuristic approximations and heavy reliance on manual processing and intuition, thus introducing plausible human error failure points and oversights, heavily impeding of assessment timelines.

On a more technical note, the long-term safety of nuclear waste repositories relies on accurately predicting how gamma radiation propagates through heterogeneous geological environments. Gamma photons interact via absorption and scattering processes that depend strongly on both the material composition and local density.

1.2 Research Motivation

In current research, two methods are considered standard in the field: Monte Carlo approaches, such as MCNP and Geant41, provide benchmark accuracy as they are mathematically built to converge towards a negligeable error margin by sampling all interaction paths; however, they scale as O(Nhistories)\mathcal{O}(N_\text{histories})O(Nhistories​) and suffer from stochastic noise, thus becoming computationally intractable for domains of larger scale (such as a city or a region).
On the opposite side of the spectrum, discretizing analytical models2 are significantly more efficient but rely on simplifications (replacing stochastic sampling with continuous flux equations) that neglect essential phenomena, rendering their usage too innacurate for any critical application.

This project aims to demonstrate a hybrid deterministic gamma transport solver that bridges these extremes. The approach would apply ray-tracing deterministic attenuation through energy-group diffusion-scattering correction, executed on a Bounding Volume Hierarchy (BVH)-accelerated voxel grid constructed from interpolated British Geological Survey (BGS) data.

Our goal is set and clear: successfully introduce and demonstrate a ray-tracing engine that replaces stochastic photon sampling with continuous differential formulation of the radiative transport equation(s), solved via block ray marching, and executable within a reasonable time. This is no obvious task, unfortunately.

1.3 Objectives

Our objectives are set and defined as follows:

  1. Build a realistic geological 3D model generator using open-source BGS data that maintains fidelity in spatially and compositionally heterogeneous media

  2. Design a deterministic ray-tracing engine computing gamma radiation attenuation and scattering through heterogeneous defined media

  3. Implement a results aggregations and post-processing module

  4. Integrate all solutions seemlessly into a web-app simulation platform for interaction and visualization

  5. Ensure complete transparency, scientific reproducibility, testing, demonstrability and ethical neutrality

  6. Converge within feasible runtime limits for real geological domains to acceptable error margins as defined by nuclear safety agencies and research

The project thus explores the boundary between analytical radiation physics, high-performance computing and computational geometry, testing the extent to which ray tracing and diffusion solvers can substitute stochastic photon histories.

2.2 Physical Interpretation

  • Streaming term (Ω⋅∇ψ\Omega \cdot \nabla \psiΩ⋅∇ψ):
    Represents the directional transport of photons through space, aka the rate of change of intensity along the photon’s beam trajectory.

  • Collision (loss) term (Σtψ\Sigma_t \psiΣt​ψ):
    Represents attenuation, a case of collisions which results in photons being removed from the beam by any type of interaction (absorption or scattering).

  • Scattering (gain) term:
    Represents all photons that are deflected into a given beam angle through scattering events (Compton, Rayleigh…). In a complete environment, it is the complement of collision loss in regards to total scattering change.

  • Source term (SSS):
    Introduces new photons into the system from external sources.

2.3 Application in our simulation context

In the case of our engine, we can express the end-goal of the simulation simply as a solver of a set of instances of LBTEs which considers gamma photon transport through geological media (rock, concrete, or waste storage materials).

Each voxel (homogenous block) in the 3D model represents a region with distinct macroscopic cross sections which can be derived from its chemical composition and density4.

The LBTE thus models how photons emitted by a nuclear waste source:

  • travel through the collection of homogeneous materials,
  • lose energy via scattering and absorbtion
  • contribute to local dose or flux fields

3. Hybrid Deterministic Formulation

Solving any non-trivial instance of LBTE directly is too computationally demanding. We therefore must approximate the total angular flux as:

ψ(r,E,Ω)≈ψdirect(r,E,Ω)+ψscattered(r,E,Ω) \psi(\mathbf{r}, E, \Omega) \approx \psi_{\text{direct}}(\mathbf{r}, E, \Omega) + \psi_{\text{scattered}}(\mathbf{r}, E, \Omega) ψ(r,E,Ω)≈ψdirect​(r,E,Ω)+ψscattered​(r,E,Ω)

Neglected terms include downscattering, secondary photon emission, and pair-production-induced radiation, which are negligeable for MeV-scale gamma energies and geological densities.

The decomposition allows different physical and numerical treatments:

  • ψdirect\psi_{\text{direct}}ψdirect​: ballistic (uncollided) photons computed deterministically by ray-tracing through the BVH voxel grid5;

  • ψscattered\psi_{\text{scattered}}ψscattered​: multiply scattered field solved via a diffusion-like approximation.

Solving for ψdirect\psi_{\text{direct}}ψdirect​ and ψscattered\psi_{\text{scattered}}ψscattered​ is yet another simplified way of expressing the problem at hand. The value of ψ(r,E,Ω)\psi(\mathbf{r}, E, \Omega)ψ(r,E,Ω) for a specific given 3D model must be computed for every point of interest within the same 3D model.

4. Geological Model Generation

The geological domain is reconstructed from BGS borehole and lithostratigraphic data, on top of which fault and dykes shapefiles are added for context clues. Data within a bounding box around user-selected coordinates are interpolated through a hybrid Kriging-Inverse Distance Weighting (IDW) function which enforces the faults and dykes as discontinuities, generating a 2-dimension cross-section

The cross-section is then extruded into 3D according to digital elevation models and stratigraphic dip angles, producing a volumetric field of lithology indices L(x,y,z)L(x, y, z)L(x,y,z) mapped to material properties: density ρ(x,y,z)\rho(x, y, z)ρ(x,y,z) and macroscopic cross-sections Σt(E,x,y,z)\Sigma_t(E, x, y, z)Σt​(E,x,y,z)4.

Validation of this reconstruction is a substantial challenge. Borehole data are sparse, anisotropic, and uncertain; cross-validation and comparison against BGS 3D geological frameworks will be used to quantify interpolation error.

5. Methodology

5.1 Overview

The simulation engine would operate on a BVH-accelerated voxel grid, a data structure in computational geometry which allows for collisions and propagation of rays in voxels with lower complexity, enabling both spatial hierarchy and deterministic integration. A general overview of the unfolding of the project would go as follows:

  1. Geological interpolation agent and material acquisition.

  2. Voxelization and BVH Construction of the geological model for ray traversal.

  3. Population and caching of the macroscopic cross-section database (Σt\Sigma_tΣt​, Σa\Sigma_aΣa​, and Σs\Sigma_sΣs​) per material and energy group.

  4. Direct Ray-Tracing Pass: compute ψdirect\psi_{\text{direct}}ψdirect​ for sampled emission points.

  5. Scattering Correction: compute ψscattered\psi_{\text{scattered}}ψscattered​ and solve diffusion equations.

  6. Validation of results (with basic tests to ensure no step went terribly wrong), export voxelized dose field and fluence for analysis through statistical models.

5.2 System Workflow

6. Validation and Testing

For a project of this scale with such a high number of modules and sub-classes, tight testing suites and procedures are necessary to ensure that everything works well on their own in a mathematical sense, in a programming sense, and interacts as expected with other functions. We shall proceed with the following unit and integration tests:

  1. Analytic Verification: test homogeneous attenuation agaisnt solved instances of Beer-Lambert.5

  2. Cross-Section Testing: ensure computed values of Σt\Sigma_tΣt​ and Σs\Sigma_sΣs​ correspond with values from tabulated data.4

  3. Integration & Benchmark Comparison: deterministic results compared to MCNP/Geant4 simulations on identical geometries (ex: phantom skull for precision) or results from previous reasearch sites which have been studied as potentially suitable for nuclear waste burial.6

  4. Energy Conservation Checks, ensuring that the energy emitted ≥\geq≥ absorbed + escaped flux.

  5. Geological Model Validation: stratigraphic boundary residuals, cross-checked with withheld borehole data.

Continuous validation principles will be applied to this project: every PR triggers unit & integration tests. No further PR can be applied until the previous one passes.

7. Ethical and Professional Considerations

The solver does not issue safety recommendations and does not provide any final answer about the safety of burial of nuclear waste in a given site. It computes physical dose and flux distributions from specified configurations, leaving interpretation to qualified nuclear engineers. All results will include uncertainty estimates derived from numerical and interpolation error analysis.

Reproducibility is ensured through open-source implementation, documented input provenance, and automated validation suites. The project aims to conform to IAEA guidance on computational nuclear safety assessment7.

8.2 Expected Outcomes

The project seeks to demonstrate that deterministic ray-tracing with BVH acceleration and diffusion coupling can provide radiation transport predictions for realistic geological environments within feasible computational budgets.

Success will be defined not by completion of an implementation, but by quantifying:

  • accuracy vs. Monte Carlo benchmarks within equivalent run-times,

  • scaling behavior with grid and energy resolution,

  • sensitivity to geological uncertainty,

  • feasibility of web-based integration for future environmental safety studies.
